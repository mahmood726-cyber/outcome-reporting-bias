We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled **"Outcome Reporting Bias Risk in 403 Cochrane Meta-Analyses: An Excess Significance Approach"** for consideration for publication in *Research Synthesis Methods*.

## Summary

Outcome reporting bias (ORB) -- the selective reporting of outcomes based on their results -- is widely acknowledged as a threat to the validity of systematic reviews, yet practical tools for screening reviews at elevated risk remain limited. In this study, we apply the Ioannidis-Trikalinos excess significance test together with three complementary statistical indicators (between-study heterogeneity, study outlier ratio, and precision asymmetry) to 403 Cochrane meta-analyses drawn from a large curated dataset. We classify reviews into Low, Moderate, and High ORB risk categories using a composite scoring framework.

## Key findings

- **One in six Cochrane reviews (16.1%) exhibits statistical patterns consistent with elevated ORB risk**, including excess significant findings, high heterogeneity, and outlying studies.
- The excess significance test identified 22.6% of reviews with more than one excess significant study beyond what statistical power would predict.
- High-risk reviews had markedly higher heterogeneity (mean I-squared 70.3% vs 14.8% in Low risk reviews), confirming that ORB-consistent patterns co-occur with unexplained between-study variability.

## Fit with Research Synthesis Methods

We believe this manuscript is well suited to *Research Synthesis Methods* for several reasons:

1. **Methodological innovation.** We extend the excess significance test from a standalone tool to a component of a composite screening framework, addressing a recognized limitation of single-indicator approaches.
2. **Large-scale empirical evidence.** Our analysis of 403 Cochrane reviews provides one of the largest applications of ORB screening to date, spanning diverse clinical areas.
3. **Practical utility.** The composite scoring system is straightforward to implement and could complement existing bias assessment tools (e.g., ORBIT, PRISMA 2020 reporting requirements) in systematic review production.
