## Abstract

**Background:** Outcome reporting bias (ORB) — the selective reporting of outcomes based on their results — threatens meta-analytic validity. While direct detection requires comparing registered to published outcomes, proxy indicators can flag reviews at higher risk.

**Methods:** We applied the Ioannidis-Trikalinos excess significance test and three complementary indicators (I-squared heterogeneity, study outlier ratio, precision asymmetry) to 403 Cochrane systematic reviews from the Pairwise70 dataset. Reviews were classified as Low, Moderate, or High ORB risk based on a composite score.

**Results:** 65.5% of reviews were Low risk, 18.4% Moderate, and 16.1% High. The excess significance test identified 22.6% of reviews with more than one excess significant study and 11.9% with more than two. High ORB risk reviews had significantly higher I-squared (mean 62% vs 28% in Low risk) and more outlying studies.

**Conclusions:** One in six Cochrane reviews shows indicators consistent with elevated outcome reporting bias risk. These reviews merit closer scrutiny of pre-registration fidelity and outcome switching.
